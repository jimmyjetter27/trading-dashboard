Strategy Notes

High-Value XAUUSD Setups

London Liquidity Sweep

Gold often raids an Asian high or low shortly after London starts, then reverses once liquidity is taken. The cleaner setups are the ones where price sweeps a level, rejects fast, and then reclaims a nearby intraday structure point.

New York Reversal

This works best when London extends too far into a premium or discount zone and New York opens into exhaustion. Look for an aggressive wick, structure shift, and then a measured retracement entry instead of chasing the first spike.

ORL-25 Expansion

When the first session M15 range is already large enough relative to daily ATR, it can signal manipulation and set up a reversal later outside the box. The best signals happen when the breakout is obvious and the reversal candle appears close to the boundary instead of deep into no-man's land.

HTF Pullback Continuation

If H4 or D1 bias is already clean, many strong gold entries come from waiting for price to pull back into a higher-timeframe zone during London or New York. This tends to be calmer than trying to fade a trend, and it usually gives you tighter stops for better RR.

Consolidation Breakout

When gold compresses through multiple 5-minute candles and keeps rejecting the same boundaries, a later break can run hard. The better breakouts usually come after several failed attempts at one side, because that tells you liquidity has built up there.

Support / Resistance Reaction

Simple levels still work when they align with session timing, structure, and momentum context. The edge is not the level alone, but the confluence of level, session, rejection quality, and whether price arrived there impulsively or slowly.

Session Timing

Best Windows To Trade

Sydney Session

Sydney is usually quieter for gold and major forex pairs. It is better for marking early range behavior than expecting explosive moves, unless there is a major macro catalyst already moving the market.

Tokyo Session

Tokyo often helps define the intraday range that London later attacks. If you trade gold, Tokyo can be useful for identifying the highs and lows that may later become liquidity targets rather than for forcing frequent entries.

London Open

London is one of the best times for volatility, structure shifts, and liquidity sweeps. For XAUUSD, many of the cleanest directional intraday moves start here, especially when London attacks an Asian range extreme and then expands decisively.

New York Open

New York can either continue London's move or reverse it sharply. This session is especially important for gold because U.S. flows, data releases, and overlap volatility can create the strongest setups of the day.

London / New York Overlap

This is usually the most active window of the day. If you want strong movement, clean confirmation, and enough volatility to hit targets quickly, this is often the best period to focus your attention.

When To Stand Aside

If price is stuck mid-range, if sessions are slow, or if your best setup has not appeared, protecting mental capital matters too. Many avoidable losses come from trading bad timing rather than from reading direction badly.

Execution Reminders

Things Worth Checking Before Entry

Is The Session Active?

A good setup during dead time can still fail because the market lacks participation. Session timing is not a bonus detail for gold; it is often part of the setup itself.

Is There Clear Liquidity?

Ask where obvious highs, lows, equal highs, or equal lows are sitting. Many strong intraday moves begin after those pools get swept.

Does RR Still Make Sense?

Even a decent idea can become a bad trade if the stop is too large or the target is too close. If the market has already done most of the move, patience is often the better trade.

Market Concepts

FVG, MSS, CHoCH, And More

Fair Value Gap (FVG)

An FVG is an imbalance left when price moves so aggressively that one candle's range does not overlap properly with the next one. In practice, traders often watch for price to revisit that gap and react before continuing in the original direction.

Why it matters: it gives you a cleaner retracement area than chasing the first expansion candle.

MSS (Market Structure Shift)

MSS is the moment price stops respecting one short-term structure direction and breaks the opposing structure point. In plain terms, it is the first serious clue that momentum has changed hands.

Why it matters: it helps you avoid fading a sweep too early without confirmation.

CHoCH (Change Of Character)

CHoCH is the first more obvious break that tells you the market's behavior has changed. Traders often use it almost interchangeably with MSS, but CHoCH usually describes the broader shift in rhythm rather than just one minor structure break.

Why it matters: it helps frame whether you are catching a deeper reversal or just a brief pullback.

Order Block

A basic order block is the last opposite candle before an impulsive move that causes displacement. The idea is that if price returns there cleanly, it can react again because that zone helped launch the original push.

Why it matters: it gives you a tighter retracement zone than entering randomly after a sweep.

Liquidity Sweep

A liquidity sweep happens when price takes an obvious high or low, triggers breakout traders or resting stops, and then quickly rejects. On gold, this is one of the cleanest ways the market creates fuel before a reversal or expansion.

Why it matters: it tells you where the market may have grabbed orders before moving the other way.

Reversal Patterns

Classic Turnaround Structures

Double Top

A double top is a bearish reversal where price tests resistance twice and fails to keep making new highs. The stronger signal is the neckline break after the second rejection.

Best use: after an extended rise into session highs, PDH, weekly highs, or round-number resistance.

Head And Shoulders

This bearish reversal shows weakening buyers in three pushes: left shoulder, higher head, and then a weaker right shoulder. A neckline break confirms the structure has rolled over.

Best use: when the right shoulder forms into resistance and lower-timeframe momentum already looks heavy.

Rising Wedge

A rising wedge often signals bullish exhaustion. Price is still climbing, but the swings compress into a narrowing structure, which can lead to a bearish break.

Best use: near resistance after a run-up, especially if a lower boundary break comes with stronger bearish candles.

Double Bottom

A double bottom is the bullish mirror of a double top. Price defends the same support area twice, then breaks above the neckline to confirm buyers have taken over.

Best use: after a decline into PDL, weekly low, demand, or a clear low sweep.

Inverse Head And Shoulders

This bullish reversal forms after a decline: left shoulder, deeper head, then a shallower right shoulder. The neckline break is what confirms the market has shifted.

Best use: when the right shoulder holds above the head and momentum starts reclaiming structure.

Falling Wedge

A falling wedge often hints that bearish momentum is fading. Price still trends lower, but the structure tightens until an upside break can trigger a bullish reversal.

Best use: at support or after a sell-off when the upper wedge line breaks with stronger bullish follow-through.
